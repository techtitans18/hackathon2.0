MEDICAL REPORT - KIDNEY STONE DIAGNOSIS

Patient Information:
Date of Examination: January 15, 2024
Chief Complaint: Severe right flank pain with hematuria

Clinical History:
The patient presented to the emergency department with acute onset of severe right-sided flank pain radiating to the groin. The pain started approximately 6 hours prior to presentation and has been progressively worsening. Associated symptoms include nausea, vomiting, and visible blood in urine. Patient reports similar episode 2 years ago which resolved spontaneously.

Physical Examination:
Vital signs stable. Temperature 37.2°C, BP 135/85 mmHg, HR 88 bpm. Patient appears uncomfortable and restless. Abdomen soft, non-distended. Marked tenderness over right costovertebral angle. No peritoneal signs. Bowel sounds normal.

Laboratory Findings:
- Urinalysis: RBC >100/hpf, WBC 5-10/hpf, pH 6.0
- Serum Creatinine: 1.1 mg/dL (normal)
- BUN: 18 mg/dL (normal)
- Calcium: 9.8 mg/dL (normal)
- Uric Acid: 6.2 mg/dL (normal)

Imaging Studies:
Non-contrast CT scan of abdomen and pelvis reveals:
- 7mm calculus in the right proximal ureter causing moderate hydronephrosis
- No evidence of ureteral perforation
- Left kidney appears normal
- No other significant findings

Diagnosis:
Right ureteral calculus (kidney stone) with associated hydronephrosis

Treatment Plan:
1. Pain management with IV ketorolac and morphine
2. Aggressive hydration with IV fluids
3. Alpha-blocker therapy (tamsulosin 0.4mg daily) to facilitate stone passage
4. Antiemetic medication as needed
5. Strain urine to capture stone for analysis
6. Follow-up imaging in 2 weeks if stone not passed
7. Urology referral if stone does not pass within 4 weeks or symptoms worsen

Patient Education:
- Increase fluid intake to 2-3 liters daily
- Avoid foods high in oxalate (spinach, nuts, chocolate)
- Return immediately if fever develops or pain becomes unmanageable
- Stone analysis will guide future prevention strategies

Prognosis:
Good. Stones of this size have approximately 70% chance of spontaneous passage within 4 weeks with medical expulsive therapy. Patient advised to follow up with primary care physician and urology as scheduled.
